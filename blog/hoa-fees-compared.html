Downtown Austin condo
HOA fees, compared.

What residents in five different buildings actually pay each month, and what their fees cover.

BY NICOLE JAMES · MARCH 15, 2026

HOA fees are the line item most downtown Austin condo buyers underweight in their budget. The brochure number and the lived-experience number are rarely the same. What follows is a look at monthly dues in five downtown buildings I know well, what those fees actually fund, and the questions I ask on every buyer’s behalf before offer.

Note: fee figures below reflect typical units in each building as of Q2 2026. Exact dues vary by unit square footage and assessments can shift year to year. These numbers are reference points, not quotes.

The five buildings

Fifth & West

A 39-story tower at 501 West Avenue. HOA dues average approximately $0.90 per square foot, with residences ranging from $587 to $2,564 per month.

The building is known for having one of the largest collections of three-bedroom residences in downtown Austin. The HOA is guided by a strong board that takes a thoughtful, measured approach to decisions that enhance the overall living experience.

The Independent

The 58-story tower on the west side of downtown. HOA dues average approximately $0.78 per square foot, with residences ranging from $543 to $2,740 per month.

The building is known for maintaining relatively stable dues over time, even while addressing larger capital projects such as the FSS system. It remains one of the more consistently well-run HOAs in downtown Austin.

That consistency in management has played a role in how units are positioned and absorbed across different market cycles.

The Austonian

A 56-story tower in downtown Austin. HOA dues average approximately $1.12 per square foot, with residences ranging from $1,300 to $5,353 per month.

The building is widely regarded as having one of the most well-run HOA boards in downtown, having maintained dues at approximately $1.00 per square foot for many years before recent increases.

Interior common spaces are meticulously maintained, and residences are defined by larger-scale, highly customized floor plans rarely seen elsewhere in the market.

The 360

A 44-story tower in the Market District. HOA dues average approximately $0.97 per square foot, with residences ranging from $693 to $2,008 per month.

Recent increases in dues have been largely driven by rising insurance costs. In response, the HOA has implemented new procedures and operational measures to better manage risk and long-term expenses.

The building has also completed a significant special assessment tied to balcony and exterior repairs, addressing structural and waterproofing needs.

Bridges on The Park

A six-story mid-rise on the south side of Lady Bird Lake. HOA dues average approximately $0.73 per square foot, with most one- and two-bedroom residences ranging from $630 to $1,170 per month.

The building is well managed, with healthy reserves that have supported its long-term performance and resale strength.

East-facing residences overlooking Butler Park and the downtown skyline are consistently sought after, with a number of transactions occurring off market.

What the fees actually cover

Every HOA disclosure lists the headline amenities. Fewer disclosures make clear what else the fee pays for. The categories to ask about on every building:

  • Insurance. Master policy on the building’s common elements and structure. Does not cover your interior.
  • Water, gas, trash. Usually bundled. Electricity is almost always individual.
  • Staffing. Concierge hours, engineering, valet if applicable, housekeeping for common areas.
  • Reserves. The line item most buyers skip. Healthy reserves mean a lower risk of special assessments. Weak reserves mean the building will come back for money, sometimes quickly.
  • Capital projects. Roof, façade, elevators, mechanical systems. Aging buildings budget for these. Newer ones defer, then assess.

The question I ask every time

Before we offer on any downtown condo, I request the last two years of HOA financials, the current reserve study if one exists, and the minutes from the most recent owner meetings. Three questions follow from that packet. What percent of reserves are funded relative to the reserve study recommendation. What the monthly dues have done over the last five years. Whether any special assessment has been passed or is pending.

Most buyers never see these documents. Most buildings’ brochures do not reference them. This is the gap between what a condo looks like and what owning one costs over a five- or ten-year hold, and it is one of the larger differences between buying a single-family home and buying a condominium.

One more thing

HOA dues are not a vice. A well-run building with sufficient fees and strong reserves is one of the better investments in Austin, because the building holds value in a way an under-funded one does not. Low dues are a red flag nearly as often as high ones. The work is reading the financials, not reacting to the number.
